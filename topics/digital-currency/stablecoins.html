This sounds like a very simple question. But as usual, the answer is not so simple …

As I have argued in my paper, CBDC needs to be interoperable with various classes of other payment systems, such as instant payment (IPS), real-time gross settlement (RTGS), but also stablecoins. Even though stablecoins and CBDC have very different operational characteristics, there is one commonality: they are typically issued by a centralized entity.

The most popular stablecoins, USDT and USDC, are issued by Tether and Circle, respectively. Those entities exert full control over the supply and cash-in/cash-out transactions. Arguably, that makes them centralized. (Note that I only consider collateral-backed stablecoins here, and not algorithmic stablecoins, which work differently.)

But USDT and USDC are available on public blockchains, like Ethereum, Tron, Solana, and others. While technical details differ across this platform, most stablecoins on most platforms are provided in the form of a smart contract according to the ERC-20 standard. That means that anyone with an Ethereum wallet can obtain Tether and transact freely. Transactions are validated through the underlying consensus mechanisms of the distributed ledger. Arguably, that makes them decentralized.

How can we reconcile these two positions? Instead of classifying stablecoins in a binary of “centralized” or “decentralized”, we need to take a more nuanced look.

Consider the underlying ledger first. As I have discussed in a previous post, cryptocurrencies rely on a decentralized network, using both a consensus algorithm that assumes no trust between participants, and an incentivization structure that rewards honest participation. Since stablecoins use the same infrastructure, this aspect applies. Specifically, third parties cannot prevent me from transacting with others.

But the stablecoin issuer can. Tether routinely freezes addresses they believe are engaged in criminal activity. This can easily be observed on Etherscan:

This is not specific to Tether; other stablecoin operators also have the ability to freeze and/or repossess holdings. Note that this is distinct from stealing private keys from someone’s wallet: it is simply a feature programmed in the smart contract. Because smart contracts are generally open source, this can be inspected by anyone. (In the case of PayPal’s PYUSD, a random programmer has even proposed a code change to remove the freezing feature.) With the advent of regulation that sanction the general concept of stablecoins, controls over how holdings can be used are here to stay.

You may now wonder: If most stablecoins have centralized governance (like CBDC), why are they using a distributed ledger? Stablecoins are often used as convenient trading vehicle for cryptocurrencies, so it makes sense to reside on the same platform. Ethereum is particularly popular for this, where a lot of different assets are managed in ERC-20 compatible contracts.

Other than that, stablecoins could also be running on different platforms. There are already some projects that explore such options, where both the issuance and the transaction aspects are controlled by the same entity. One such example are deposit tokens. Options here are private blockchains or fully centralized systems.

Conclusion

Most major stablecoins are issued by a centralized entity, which control the supply and cash transactions. However, these stablecoins are available on public blockchains, like Ethereum, where transactions are validated through consensus mechanisms, suggesting a decentralized aspect. This suggests that we should take a more nuanced look at stablecoins. For example, while the stablecoin issuer can freeze addresses suspected of criminal activity, third parties cannot prevent transactions. Because the use of stablecoins is expanding beyond a trading vehicle for cryptocurrencies, some projects are exploring more centralized approaches, removing the reliance on public blockchains.
